How to Get Started building a Textbook

Source: https://commons.libretexts.org/insight/how-to-get-started
Last updated at 09/03/2025 06:04 PM by Jake Turner

Description: This is the five step workflow to get started in building a LibreTexts textbook.

The steps to build or remix a new textbook on the LibreTexts libraries is fairly straightforward but involves a few steps to get started.

Step 1: Obtain a LibreOne account at https://register.libretexts.org. 

We kindly ask that instructors sign up with their institutional email address to speed up the instructor verification process. You may also create an account by signing in with Google or Microsoft.

 

Step 2: Complete instructor verification

During the LibreOne registration process, you will be prompted to submit your instructor verification request. If you are not prompted, or need to return to this later, you can always do so at https://one.libretexts.org/instructor. A decisions are usually made quite quickly, but please allow up to 48 hours for this. For more information on instructor verification and who is eligible, please see this article: https://commons.libretexts.org/insight/instructor-verification

 

stopStop here until your verification is approved! Without verification, you will not be able to create or edit content on the LibreTexts libraries. stop 

 

Step 3: Create a new Conductor Project

Once your instructor verification is approved, you automatically have access to most of the 'LibreVerse'. You can view all of our apps and libraries by visiting the 'Launchpad' at https://launchpad.libretexts.org. From the Launchpad, you can access Conductor, which is LibreTexts' project management tool (and, in fact, the only one available that was built specifically for OER construction)

Once signed in Conductor, click the large green "Create Conductor Project" button. When clicked, all you need to add is the title of the project and select if you want the project to be 'public' (so other people can find the project - but not read what is going inside). The title of your project does not need to match what the title of your book will be, though keeping them the same can make organization easier if you have many projects.

 

 

Step 4: Create new textbook template

Within your Conductor project, create a new (empty) textbook template. If you are working on a non-text project (e.g., homework or ancillary materials) ignore Step 3-5. Creating a new text will create an empty book in the 'workbench'. This is where books that are a work in progress are held until officially 'published' (this used to be known as a sandbox).

One created, a "Textbook Link" appears that will be the link to the book. 

Step 5: Edit and remix as needed

The empty book can now be edited or remixed as desired.

The OER Remixer is designed for managing (editing, deleting, moving, etc.) of pages. To access the remixer - the user interface for easy remixing and organization - click the pull down menu on the purple "More Tools" button on the Conductor page. This will open the OER Remixer and automatically set your new book as the target for your changes.

More details on how to remix can be found in Chapter 7 of the LibreTexts Construction Guide.

The page editor is the tool for editing content on a specific page. That is accessed on each page on the top black navigation bar. If the bar is grey, then you need to sign into the library in upper right corner of the page using the LibreOne account. If the bar is blue, then you have a student account and will need to contact us for assistance by submitting a support ticket at https://commons.libretexts.org/support/contact

More details on editing of page content can be found in Chapters 3 and 4 of the LibreTexts Construction Guide.

 

Step 6: Publish and Compile

Once complete (or once you are ready to show the text to the world) request the text to be "Published" (reviewed and migrated into the applicable Campus bookshelf or central bookshelf). At this point, the book is "Compiled" (export options generated including PDF, Common Cartridge, Bookstore entry, and cataloged in the Commons system). 

Once a book is published, authors still have full rights to edit as they see fit. Editing does not necessarily stop at publishing - though it is recommended to keep edits minor. If you want to do a complete rewrite of your text, we recommend following these same steps to create a second edition instead.
