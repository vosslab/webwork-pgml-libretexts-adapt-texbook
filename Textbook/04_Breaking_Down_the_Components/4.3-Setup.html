Setup

Setup is where a general prompt becomes a specific, randomized instance. This is where you define values, choose realistic ranges, compute derived quantities, and create answer objects for the blanks in the Statement.

In life-science prompts, realism is not cosmetic. The ranges and step sizes should reflect what a student could actually pipette, interpret, or report in a lab context.

Debug loop: if a particular seed makes the problem nonsensical, fix the constraints here rather than rewriting the prompt. The fastest maintenance wins come from tightening the generator, not from adding more text.

Why this exists: it turns a story into randomized values and answer objects that the PGML can use.

Common edits Common failures
  • Adjust ranges, units, or constraints.
  • Change tolerances or grading rules.
  • Random values create impossible wet-lab variants.
  • Units drift between setup and prompt.

Try it now: change one range, render three seeds, and scan for unit drift or an impossible pipetting value. If you see a problem, tighten the constraint here rather than rewriting the prompt.

Variables and constraints

Variable Range Constraint Why it exists
c_stock 5 to 20 μM Multiples of 5 Keeps pipetting values realistic.
v_stock 1 to 5 μL Whole microliters Avoids fractional pipetting in wet lab.
v_final 20 to 50 μL Multiples of 5 Standard reaction volumes.

Computed targets are the glue between Setup and Statement: these are the answer objects that will be attached to blanks in the prompt.

Grading knobs (tolerance patterns)

Where to set it What it controls When to use it
On cmp() Per-answer tolerance (absolute or relative). One answer needs a custom tolerance.
On Context() Defaults for the entire problem. Most answers share the same tolerance rules.
On Compute() Explicit rounding to a target format. You want to lock a specific display format.

For tolerance details, follow the NumericalTolerance sample problem in the OpenWeBWorK PG samples and mirror its three-option pattern (cmp, Context, Compute).

Life-science constraints matter most here: keep units consistent, choose plausible ranges, and avoid variants that would be impossible in a wet lab.
