Statement

The Statement is the student-facing prompt written in PGML. It is where the story, data, and tasks become visible, and where each blank must be tied to one answer object from Setup.

Think of the Statement as a contract. The text and units you show here must match what the checker accepts, or students will feel the grading is arbitrary even when the math is right. Example: the prompt says μM but the checker expects mM or unitless input.

Choose the structure that reduces reading load: use short paragraphs for a single task, lists for steps, and tables for multi-value data. If a prompt feels hard to scan, structure is usually the fix, not more words.

Why this exists: it is the student-facing prompt that ties the story to the answer blanks.

Common edits Common failures
  • Rewrite the story in plain language.
  • Add or reorder blanks and labels.
  • Blank not attached to an answer object.
  • Variable name does not match Setup.

Fast test: render one seed, enter the expected answer, confirm grading, then change the seed and repeat.

Use tables only when there is real data to scan or compare, not to simulate layout. Lists and short paragraphs are usually better for narrative prompts.

Structure patterns

Pattern When to use Avoid when
Short paragraphs One clear story with 1 to 2 computations. Steps are procedural or multi-part.
Lists Protocols or multi-step directions. The prompt is a single sentence.
Tables Real data or comparisons need scanning. Two values can be in plain text.
Inline math Small substitutions and unit checks. Equations are long or multi-line.
